How I Built the SME Credit Decision Tool

A full-stack loan underwriting tool for SME lenders. Enter a business's financials, get back a risk score, DSCR analysis, automated credit flags, amortization schedule, and three loan scenarios to compare.

Try it live

The Idea

SME lending decisions are often made in spreadsheets, with analysts manually calculating DSCR, looking up industry benchmarks, and flagging risks in a notes field. Having worked in regulated financial services, I wanted to model what a purpose-built credit underwriting tool could look like - one where the scoring logic is transparent, auditable, and consistent across every application.

The goal was a tool an SME lender's credit team could actually use: enter the basics, get a defensible recommendation with the reasoning broken out, not just a number.

The Credit Engine

DSCR is the primary underwriting signal - but the final score is a 5-factor weighted model that accounts for margin quality, business maturity, existing leverage, and industry risk alongside cash flow coverage.

The scoring model runs entirely on the server, so the weights and logic are never exposed to the client:

FactorWeightHow it's scored
DSCR Coverage35%Linear scale capped at 2.5x
Debt-to-Revenue15%Lower is better; 40%+ scores zero
Margin Quality15%Piecewise: below 10% / 15% / 25%
Business Maturity15%Non-linear sqrt curve on years trading
Industry Risk20%Lookup table across 10 sectors

The composite score feeds a continuous interest rate calculation - rather than banding into fixed tiers, the rate adjusts smoothly with the score, with further discounts for business tenure and industry adjustments layered on top.

Loan sizing is constrained by two independent caps: a cash-flow cap (available operating income after existing debt, with a 1.25x DSCR buffer) and a revenue multiple cap (max 3x annual revenue). The lower of the two becomes the maximum lendable amount.

Key Features

Loan application form

Business financials input: monthly revenue, operating margin, existing debt, years trading, sector, and requested loan details.

Risk gauge

Visual dial showing the composite risk score and LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH classification with the DSCR value front and centre.

Credit flags

Automated warnings (thin margin, high leverage), cautions (startup risk, marginal DSCR, concentration), and positive indicators (strong cash flow, low leverage).

Loan scenarios

Three alternative structures generated automatically: conservative, recommended, and stretch - each with monthly payment, DSCR impact, and interest rate.

Amortization schedule

Full month-by-month breakdown of principal, interest, and outstanding balance, with a Recharts area chart showing the paydown curve.

Portfolio exposure

Separate view tracking approved loans across the book: sector concentration, risk distribution, and total exposure - the view a credit manager would use.

How It Would Work in Production

The current version takes manual inputs. In a real lending context, most of that data would arrive automatically:

  • Open banking - revenue and cash flow verified directly from bank feeds via Plaid, TrueLayer, or Yapily, rather than self-reported figures
  • Companies House - years in business, registered sector, and director history pulled automatically on company number lookup
  • Credit bureau data - Experian or Equifax commercial scores layered in as an additional input to the risk model, replacing or supplementing the manual margin input
  • Existing debt verification - HMRC filing data or accountant-certified management accounts for debt obligations, removing the self-reported element that creates the most model risk
  • Decision audit log - every application, score, and decision stored immutably for FCA compliance and fair lending review

The credit engine itself - the scoring weights, flag thresholds, and approval logic - would be tuned against the lender's actual book performance over time. The architecture keeps the engine entirely server-side and separate from the UI precisely so it can be iterated on, A/B tested, or replaced without touching the frontend.

What I Learned

  • Designing a weighted scoring model with transparent, auditable factor breakdowns rather than a black-box number
  • Full-stack monorepo structure with npm workspaces - keeping client and server in sync without over-engineering the setup
  • How loan sizing constraints work in practice: cash flow cap, revenue multiple cap, and tier-specific minimum DSCR thresholds
  • The difference between what a credit model needs to be correct and what it needs to be trusted - presentation and explainability matter as much as the maths
  • Deploying an Express API as Vercel serverless functions with a separate React client in the same repo
